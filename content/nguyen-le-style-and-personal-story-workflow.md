# Nguyen Le's Writing Style + A Workflow for Personal-Story Emails

Based on reading his emails (Verse Co / Process Masterclass), grouped into the personal, non-selling ones you want to learn from: "Life and work," "There's a folder on my desktop," "Hello 2026," "An incredible 2025," and "A small dream just shipped."

---

## Part 1: How he writes

### Voice
- Warm, plain, and personal. Talks like a friend catching you up, not a marketer.
- Confident but humble. He shares wins ($1.8M, 187% growth) and losses ($52k in NFTs gone, his hardest year) in the same breath.
- Reflective. Most emails end on a life lesson, not a pitch.

### Sentence and paragraph rhythm
- Short paragraphs. Often one or two sentences each.
- Heavy white space. The eye never hits a wall of text.
- One idea per line when he wants weight:
  > "Your path does not need to be fast to be meaningful.
  > Your growth does not need to be dramatic to be real.
  > Your progress does not need to impress anyone to matter."
- Fragments for punch. "Ten years." "Who knew." "It's been a long journey."

### What makes it land
- **Specificity.** Real names, real numbers, real objects. $10 first Gumroad sale. Three-dollar Subway lunches. A folder called "Student Success." His son Theo and the Rubik's cube. Specifics make it true.
- **Vulnerability.** He admits fear before every cohort. He names grief over his dad. He calls failed bets "tuition." This is the engine of trust.
- **Scene-setting openers.** He drops you into a moment:
  > "It's late, the kids are finally asleep, and I've got the Claude Code app open."
  > "I'm currently typing this on my MacBook at the family kitchen table as I watch my kids watch TV."
- **The turn to you.** After the story, he pivots to the reader: "Maybe you feel some of those days in your career too." Then a question: "How can 2026 be a knockout year for you?"
- **Quotes as anchors.** Seneca, his dad, a student's Slack message. Borrowed weight.

### How he handles selling
This is the key lesson for your no-sell sends.
- In a **pure personal email**, there is no ask. It closes with gratitude: "Thanks for being here. Appreciate you."
- When he **does** sell, the story comes first and runs long. The pitch arrives at the very end, often after the sign-off, in a P.S. He even adds an opt-out: "Not interested in the workshop? No hard feelings, click here and I'll stop."
- The story is never bait. It stands on its own. The offer is a footnote to a message that already gave value.

### Signature tics
- Opens with "Hey [name],"
- Closes with "Cheers, Nguyen"
- Sparing, deliberate swearing for emphasis ("a f**king great year").
- Emoji almost never, and only ":)".

## Part 2: His structure for a personal, non-selling email

A repeatable six-part shape. Every personal email he sends follows some version of this.

1. **The hook** — a concrete scene or a curious line. Present tense works well. ("There's a folder on my desktop. It's called Student Success.")
2. **The story** — one specific thing that happened. A message from a student. A trip. A memory. Keep it to one story, told in detail.
3. **The honest middle** — the messy or hard part. What scared you, what failed, what you doubted. Don't skip this. It's why people keep reading.
4. **The turn** — zoom out to the lesson or principle the story points to.
5. **The bridge to the reader** — "Maybe you've felt this too." Or a direct question to them.
6. **The warm close** — gratitude, no ask. "Thanks for being here."

## Part 3: Your workflow for writing these

### Note on your own rules
Borrow Nguyen's *structure and vulnerability*, but keep your surface style. You avoid em dashes, semicolons, emojis, and asterisks. He uses em dashes and ":)" freely. So: steal the bones, not the punctuation. Where he writes "design has given me everything — a career, a business," you write "Design gave me everything. A career. A business."

### Step 1: Build a story bank
You can't write personal emails on demand if you have no raw material. Keep a running list. Add to it whenever something happens. Pull from it when it's time to write.

Sources that work:
- A message or reply from a reader or customer.
- A small moment with family or a friend.
- A mistake you made and what it cost.
- A win you weren't sure would happen.
- A thing you're scared of right now.
- A memory the present moment reminded you of.

For each, jot three things: what happened, the specific detail, the lesson it points to.

### Step 2: Pick one story and one lesson
One email, one story, one takeaway. Don't stack three lessons. Nguyen's best emails make a single point.

### Step 3: Draft against the six-part shape
Hook, story, honest middle, turn, bridge, close. Write fast and ugly first.

### Step 4: Cut for white space
Break long paragraphs into one or two sentences. Add line breaks between ideas. Read it on your phone. If any block feels heavy, split it.

### Step 5: Run the pre-send checklist
- Does it open with a concrete scene or a curious line, not a windup?
- Is there one specific detail only you could have written? (A name, a number, an object.)
- Did I show something real, including the hard part?
- Does it turn to the reader at least once?
- Is there genuinely no ask, or is the ask a quiet footnote?
- Did I cut every word I could remove without losing the point?

## Part 4: Fill-in templates

### Template A: The "small moment" email (no ask)

> Hey [name],
>
> [One sentence dropping the reader into a scene. Present tense. Concrete.]
>
> [Two or three sentences telling the one thing that happened.]
>
> [The honest part. What you felt, doubted, or got wrong.]
>
> [The turn. The lesson the moment points to, said plainly.]
>
> [The bridge. "Maybe you've felt this too." Or a question to them.]
>
> Thanks for being here.
>
> [Your name]

### Template B: The reflection email (anchored by a quote or memory)

> Hey [name],
>
> [A question or observation that sets up the theme.]
>
> [A short personal memory or a quote that frames it.]
>
> [Your story. Specific. One thread.]
>
> [What it taught you.]
>
> [Three short lines, one idea each, building to the point.]
>
> [A direct question to the reader about their own life or year.]
>
> [Your name]

### Template C: The story-first email with a quiet offer

Use this when you do want to mention something, without it feeling like a sale.

> Hey [name],
>
> [Hook + full personal story, told for its own sake. This is 80% of the email.]
>
> [The lesson.]
>
> [The bridge to the reader.]
>
> [One short paragraph: "If this is something you're working on, I made [thing]. Here's the link." Keep it small.]
>
> [Your name]
>
> P.S. [Optional opt-out or a second light nudge. Never the main event.]
