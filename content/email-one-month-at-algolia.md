Subject: One month in at Algolia

Hey [name],

A month ago I walked into a new job for the first time in a while. Staff Product Designer at Algolia, on Agent Studio. It's the platform that lets businesses build and deploy AI agents grounded in their own data. I'm the only designer on it, across three engineering teams.

I wanted this one specifically.

Most design roles now are versions of work I'd already done. This one sits where the industry is heading. Designing for AI that behaves differently every time it runs. Confidence signals. Guardrails. What the interface does when the model gets it wrong. Most designers haven't touched that yet. I wanted to be early.

Getting here was not clean.

I interviewed at seven companies over four months. I rebuilt my portfolio more times than I'd like to admit. I drilled my answers until I could deliver each one in under two minutes. For Algolia, I built a working prototype of one of their features and demoed it live in the interview.

The rejections stung.

One hiring manager sent detailed feedback. Portfolio too long. Unclear how much of the design was mine versus the AI tool's defaults. Not enough storytelling at my level. All accurate. All hard to read. Another process went quiet after a strong round and never came back.

That feedback was the turning point.

I stopped trying to impress with volume. I cut the portfolio down. I named the specific decisions I made on every project. I learned to stop talking once I'd made my point. What held me back wasn't my experience. I was burying it.

The first month has been a lot.

The team is further behind on AI than I expected. That sounds like a problem. It's the reason I'm here. In week one my manager told me I'd already gone further with this than most of the team, and she wanted me pushing their thinking. That's the part that made it feel worth it. I spent two years building this on weekends. Now it's the job.

Here's what I keep coming back to.

Rejection isn't a verdict on you. It's information. Seven processes, a stack of nos, and the one that mattered landed because I used what the nos taught me.

If you're in the middle of that right now, keep going. The feedback that stings the most is usually the most useful.

John

P.S. If you're job hunting and want to compare notes, just reply. Happy to share what worked.
